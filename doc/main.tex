\begin{abstract}
This paper develops a state-contingent partial-liquidation mechanism for collateralized lending in which execution is triggered by LLTV/LTV conditions and liquidation size is computed from target-HF repair requirements. We formalize the mechanism, derive event-level accounting identities, and evaluate behavior in a reproducible multi-loan simulation framework with trigger-level execution logs. We compare fixed-close-factor liquidation, target-HF partial liquidation without buyback, and target-HF buyback under lot-matched, positive-spread, reborrow-funded execution. Across a real-window stress batch and rolling historical-window backtests, target-HF partial liquidation robustly outperforms fixed-close-factor liquidation on borrower loss (winning in $24/24$ swept candidates and $13/16$ active windows) while keeping the position solvent and incurring zero bad debt, in contrast to the fixed-close-factor baseline. For the buyback leg we identify a re-leverage failure mode: a naive reborrow-to-LLTV buyback induces ping-pong re-liquidation and \emph{increases} borrower loss. We close this with an explicit health-factor floor on buyback reborrow, after which buyback becomes a safe restoration leg that never triggers extra liquidation and consistently reduces borrower opportunity-cost loss while restoring more collateral. The benefit is robust in direction across two stress regimes: on real sampled ETH windows it is modest (mean loss reduction $\$47.6$, paired Wilcoxon $p=5.4\times10^{-11}$, $54/57$ informative pairs) and on synthetic deep-crash paths it is large ($\$1{,}090$, $77/77$ informative pairs, $p=1.2\times10^{-14}$); restoration of collateral improves in both ($p=5.1\times10^{-17}$ on real windows). The contribution is an auditable mechanism---including the re-leverage guard---and a fully reproducible evaluation protocol that make liquidation-restoration-risk trade-offs explicit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Liquidation is central to solvency in overcollateralized lending. Most deployed systems are built around one or a small number of hard CR thresholds, where falling below the threshold triggers immediate liquidation pressure. In highly volatile markets, this can create cliff effects: borrowers experience abrupt collateral loss, while protocols face concentrated execution risk.

This paper proposes \emph{state-contingent two-sided partial liquidation}, a rule-based alternative that replaces fixed-quantity bucket rules with state-driven actions. As collateral value declines, liquidation is triggered by LTV/LLTV breaches and sized by a dynamic close factor; when risk normalizes, buyback is funded through constrained reborrowing. The objective is not to eliminate liquidation, but to transform it from a discontinuous event into a staged control policy with auditable behavior.

Hard-threshold lending liquidations are widely documented in production protocols such as Aave, where positions are monitored by health-factor style constraints and liquidators repay debt for discounted collateral claims \cite{aave-liquidations}. Target-HF partial-liquidation sizing---where debt repayment is computed to restore the borrower's health factor to a governance-set target rather than applying a fixed close factor---has been formally adopted in Aave v4's liquidation engine \cite{aave-v4-liquidations} and is under active exploration in Morpho-style designs. Recent analysis of Aave v4 configurations highlights a fundamental trade-off in single-directional Target-HF systems: parameters must either favor borrower protection (resulting in low surplus and potential liveness risks during stress) or liquidation efficiency (providing high protocol revenue and execution reliability, but inflicting maximum penalties on undercollateralized borrowers) \cite{aave-v4-spoke-revenue}. Furthermore, these implementations generally stop at sell-side deleveraging and do not include an endogenous protocol buyback leg for collateral restoration. In contrast, Curve's crvUSD design introduces soft-liquidation ranges through LLAMMA, where collateral is gradually converted across price bands rather than liquidated in one step \cite{curve-crvusd,curve-llamma}.

This paper takes target-HF sizing as a shared starting point and contributes the \emph{buyback restoration} dimension: a lot-matched, positive-spread, reborrow-funded mechanism that allows partial recovery of previously liquidated collateral when market conditions improve. Crucially, we show that the restoration leg is only safe when reborrowing is bounded by a health-factor floor; without it, the buyback re-levers the position back toward the trigger and induces ping-pong re-liquidation that harms the borrower. Specifically, the study contributes in four dimensions: (i) a formal LLTV/LTV-based partial-liquidation rule with target-HF repair sizing and explicit sell/recovery triggers, including the feasibility condition $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}$ under which the repair is well posed; (ii) a \emph{health-factor-floor re-leverage guard} on the buyback leg that provably preserves a solvency buffer and eliminates ping-pong re-liquidation; (iii) a transparent accounting decomposition of debt reduction, reserve accumulation, and collateral restoration for pathwise stress diagnostics; and (iv) a fully reproducible event-level simulation pipeline---including the table- and statistics-generation scripts---that supports paired policy comparison under identical paths.

\section{Model Setup}
Consider a loan with debt $D$ (USD), collateral amount $C$ in a generic risky asset, initial asset price $P_0$, and initial collateral ratio $\mathrm{CR}_0>1$.
Initial collateral amount is
\[
C_0 = \frac{D\,\mathrm{CR}_0}{P_0}.
\]

Let LLTV denote the liquidation LTV threshold. For a single-collateral position with debt $D_t$ and collateral value $C_tP_t$, define
\[
\mathrm{LTV}_t = \frac{D_t}{C_tP_t}, \qquad
\mathrm{HF}_t = \frac{C_tP_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t}=\frac{\mathrm{LT}}{\mathrm{LTV}_t}.
\]
Liquidation eligibility is state-defined: a sell action is considered when $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge \mathrm{LLTV}$ (equivalently when HF enters the liquidation region implied by LT/LLTV calibration).

\paragraph{Endogenous partial liquidation via LLTV/LTV.}
In the current implementation, partial liquidation is sized to restore the position toward a target health factor $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}$, rather than using a fixed liquidation amount. When $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge \mathrm{LLTV}$, the engine computes the debt reduction required to move the post-trade state to the target region. With liquidation bonus $b\ge0$, sell quantity is
\[
q_t^{\text{sell}}=\frac{(1+b)\Delta D_t}{P_t},
\]
and the target-HF condition is
\[
\mathrm{HF}^{\star}=\frac{\left(C_t-\frac{(1+b)\Delta D_t}{P_t}\right)P_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t-\Delta D_t}.
\]
Solving gives the target repayment proposal
\[
\Delta D_t^{\star}=\frac{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}D_t-\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t}{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b)},
\]
which is then clipped by debt and available collateral constraints. Using $\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t=\mathrm{HF}_t D_t$, the proposal admits the equivalent, more interpretable form
\[
\Delta D_t^{\star}=\frac{D_t\,(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)}{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b)},
\]
so the trade size scales with the health-factor gap $(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)$. The repair is well posed only when both factors are positive: the numerator requires $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{HF}_t$ (the position is below target), and at the trigger boundary $\mathrm{LTV}_t=\mathrm{LLTV}$ this becomes the design feasibility condition
\[
\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\frac{\mathrm{LT}}{\mathrm{LLTV}},
\]
while the denominator requires $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}(1+b)$. If these fail, the target is infeasible and the engine falls back to full repayment. (For example, $\mathrm{LLTV}=0.85$, $\mathrm{LT}=0.83$ gives $\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}=0.976$, so any $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}\ge 1$ is feasible; conversely $\mathrm{LLTV}=0.72$ would require $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>1.153$, and a nominal $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}=1.05$ would never fire.)

If price continues to fall after the first repair trade, the position can re-enter the liquidation region and trigger a second partial liquidation. The sequence of discrete partial liquidations naturally generates an endogenous empirical price band---e.g., $(P_{t_1}, P_{t_2})$---with an exact recorded capacity of executed quantities $(q_{t_1}^{\text{sell}}, q_{t_2}^{\text{sell}})$. This path-dependent memory is critical for safely unwinding the position during a market rebound. A fundamental risk in collateral restoration is ``ping-pong liquidation'', where debt-funded buybacks immediately degrade the health factor and trigger secondary liquidations. Because a reborrow-funded buyback adds equal USD amounts of collateral and debt, it necessarily \emph{lowers} the health factor; lot-matching to the historical grid is therefore necessary but, as our experiments show (\S\ref{sec:results}), not sufficient to prevent ping-pong. We make the buyback strictly lot-matched---each recovery buy is mapped to an open previously sold lot (partial matching allowed), executed only at a positive grid spread---and we additionally bound the reborrow by a health-factor floor (below) so that the marginal re-leveraging cannot push the position back toward the liquidation trigger.

Buyback is enabled under a risk-guard condition, implemented as
\[
\mathrm{LTV}_t\le \mathrm{LLTV}+\delta,
\]
where $\delta\ge0$ is a buyback risk-guard bandwidth. For an open sell lot $i$ with quantity $q_i^{\text{sell}}$ and sell price $P_i^{\text{sell}}$, buyback executes only at a positive spread of at least $s\ge0$,
\[
P_i^{\text{sell}} > (1+s)\,P_t,
\]
and the executed buyback quantity is lot-constrained, $0 < q_t^{\text{buy}} \le q_i^{\text{sell}}$, with the aggregate target capped by buyback ratio $\eta\in(0,1]$ via $Q_t^{\text{bought,total}}\le\eta Q_t^{\text{sold,total}}$.

\paragraph{Health-factor-floor re-leverage guard.}
The key safety constraint is on \emph{how much} can be reborrowed. A naive implementation reborrows up to the LLTV capacity, $\,\mathrm{collateral}\cdot P_t\cdot\mathrm{LLTV}-D_t$, which drives the post-buy LTV straight back to LLTV and re-arms the trigger. Instead, we cap the reborrow so that the post-buy health factor stays above a floor $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}>\mathrm{LT}$. Since a buyback of USD value $x$ sets $C\mapsto C+x/P_t$ and $D\mapsto D+x$, the post-buy health factor $\mathrm{HF}=\mathrm{LT}(C_tP_t+x)/(D_t+x)\ge\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$ holds iff
\[
x \le B_t^{\star}=\max\!\left(0,\ \frac{\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t-\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}D_t}{\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}-\mathrm{LT}}\right).
\]
This guarantees a solvency buffer after every recovery buy and is what eliminates ping-pong re-liquidation; without it, lot-matching and positive spread alone do not (\S\ref{sec:results}).

Relative to a stricter guard $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}-\delta$, using $\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$ is intended to activate restoration earlier during rebounds and to reduce missed recovery opportunities in choppy regimes. This choice introduces an overlap interval $[\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta]$ where sell and buy conditions can both hold in principle. In implementation, conflict is resolved deterministically by execution order: sell logic is evaluated first, and buyback is checked only on the post-sell state within the same step.

\section{Mechanism and Accounting}
The mechanism maintains an execution state and inventory record per position. A sell event is triggered by an LLTV breach ($\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$); its size is computed online from the target-HF repair requirement, debt, and price. A buy event is governed by a risk guard ($\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$), and executes only if it strictly matches an open sell lot under positive expected grid spread and reborrow-capacity constraints. In terminal states, the policy may allow loss-making buyback during sufficiently strong recovery; this preserves explicit restoration semantics rather than purely myopic profit maximization.

Operationally, each sell event includes a liquidation bonus buffer. Let gross sell proceeds be $q_t^{\text{sell}}P_t$ and debt reduction be $\Delta D_t$; then reserve increment is $\Delta U_t=q_t^{\text{sell}}P_t-\Delta D_t$. This buffer is the first-loss-absorbing source for subsequent buyback operations and can be interpreted as the economic budget that funds recovery commitments. Consequently, occasional terminal buyback losses are not treated as model failure, but as bounded drawdowns against cumulative liquidation-buffer income.

Because the mechanism has both downward (sell) and upward (buyback) actions, execution can be permissionless on both sides. In practice, this implies two directions of keeper/liquidator participation: one side competes to execute debt-reducing sell liquidations, while the other side competes to execute inventory-restoring buybacks when recovery triggers are met. This two-sided design reduces reliance on a privileged actor and improves liveness under volatile conditions.

Protocol USD inventory evolves from sell and buy events, and borrower collateral inventory evolves inversely. Collateral value at time $t$ is
\[
V_t = C_t P_t + U_t,
\]
where $C_t$ is borrower collateral amount and $U_t$ is protocol-held USD from prior sells. The realized CR is $\mathrm{CR}_t = V_t / D$.

\paragraph{Risk trade-offs and interpretation.}
Event-level realized protocol P\&L is
\[
\Pi_k = q_k(P_k^{\text{sell}} - P_k^{\text{buy}}),
\]
and total P\&L is $\Pi=\sum_k \Pi_k$ over completed sell-buy cycles (including terminal recovery if executed).

To make tail effects explicit, we decompose total outcome as
\[
\Pi_{\text{total}} = \sum_{k\in \mathcal{K}_{\text{normal}}} \Pi_k + \Pi_{\text{terminal buyback}},
\]
where the first term is typically positive under repeated two-sided execution, while the terminal term can be negative in adverse rebound paths.

Accordingly, the mechanism should be interpreted as conditionally profitable rather than deterministically profitable: for a given parameterization and market path, expected net P\&L can be positive, but positive profit is not guaranteed in every realization.

Two properties are central:

\noindent\textbf{Path dependence} is fundamental: identical terminal prices can generate materially different outcomes if crossing order and dwell time differ. \textbf{Restoration--releverage trade-off} is equally central: larger recovery-side buybacks improve borrower-side restoration but require debt re-expansion within borrowing-capacity limits.

This mechanism therefore reallocates liquidation risk over time instead of concentrating it at a single boundary.

\section{Simulation Framework and Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}
This section reports a unified, fully reproducible evaluation pipeline for the implemented mechanism. The objective is mechanism validation under path uncertainty: we evaluate borrower outcomes, solvency/tail risk, and execution behavior under both a real-window stress batch and rolling historical windows. All tables and statistics in this section are regenerated from the run outputs by a single script (\S\ref{sec:repro}).

\subsection{Experimental design and policy set}
All experiments compare three policies under matched initial conditions and matched price paths:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{traditional\_fixed\_cf}: fixed close-factor liquidation without buyback,
  \item \texttt{target\_hf\_no\_buyback}: target-HF partial liquidation with buyback disabled,
  \item proposed target-HF with buyback (scenario key \texttt{baseline\_dynamic}): target-HF partial liquidation with strict buyback.
\end{itemize}
For the dynamic policy, buyback is constrained to lot-matched, positive-spread, reborrow-funded execution under the health-factor-floor re-leverage guard of \S2. Specifically, each buy must match an open sell lot, execute only at a positive grid spread, and respect the reborrow cap $B_t^{\star}$ that keeps the post-buy health factor above $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$. This keeps restoration behavior auditable, avoids reserve-funded shortcuts, and prevents the re-leverage from re-arming the liquidation trigger. Each loan is initialized at a controlled $\mathrm{CR}_0$ at its own start price ($C_0=D\,\mathrm{CR}_0/P_0$), so that policies are compared on identically healthy starting positions and stress is driven by the price path rather than by initial-condition heterogeneity.

\subsection{State variables, metrics, and implementation alignment}
At each step, the simulator records
\[
\mathrm{HF}_t = \frac{C_tP_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t},\qquad
\mathrm{LTV}_t = \frac{D_t}{C_tP_t},\qquad
\mathrm{CR}_t = \frac{C_tP_t}{D_t}.
\]
Sell triggers are evaluated at $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$ and buyback guard at $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$. Event logs include debt repayment, collateral sold/bought, reserve changes, and trigger-level snapshots.

Primary evaluation metrics are borrower final loss, restoration ratio $\rho=C_T/C_0$, peak bad debt $\max_t\max(0,D_t-C_tP_t)$, event turnover (sell/buy intensity), and minimum health factor. Borrower final loss is measured as an \emph{opportunity cost relative to passive holding}, $\max(0,\,C_0P_T-D_0-(C_TP_T-D_T))$: it captures how much worse the borrower is, marked at the terminal price $P_T$, than if the initial collateral had simply been held. Under this metric the value of buyback is precise---by re-acquiring previously sold collateral, it lets the borrower participate in rebounds and reduces missed upside. We report both central tendency and tail-sensitive statistics to avoid selecting policies that only win in average-case regimes.

Key configuration dimensions are as follows: loan notional and initial CR define initial position scale; LLTV and target-HF levels control trigger and repair intensity; close-factor/liquidation-bonus sets determine liquidation aggressiveness; buyback ratio and bandwidth determine recovery-side activation; and random-seed/path-window settings govern stress-batch and historical-window sampling. Exact values are scenario-dependent and are reported together with each result table/sweep output.

Two patterns are clear. First, both target-HF policies dominate the fixed-close-factor baseline on the central borrower outcome: median borrower loss falls from $2{,}753$ USD (fixed CF) to $0$ USD (target-HF, i.e.\ more than half of runs incur no opportunity-cost loss), the minimum health factor stays above $1$ ($1.027$ vs.\ $0.98$ for fixed CF, which dips into the insolvency region), collateral restoration $\rho$ is higher ($0.594$ vs.\ $0.584$), and worst-case bad debt is $0$ USD for target-HF against $27{,}624$ USD for fixed CF. Target-HF thus deleverages more smoothly while eliminating the protocol's bad-debt exposure. Second, adding the guarded buyback further lowers mean borrower loss from $13{,}303$ to $13{,}255$ USD and raises restoration from $0.5922$ to $0.5944$: the per-run effect is modest (many windows tie, since buyback only matters when a sell is followed by a rebound before the window ends) but it is strongly one-directional---buyback never triggers an extra liquidation. On the $600$ paired runs there are $57$ informative (non-tie) pairs, of which buyback reduces loss in $54$ and increases it in $3$; the one-sided paired Wilcoxon test gives $p=5.4\times10^{-11}$ that buyback \emph{reduces} loss, with bootstrap $95\%$ CI for the mean reduction equal to $[33.4,\,62.9]$ USD. The restoration-ratio gain is even cleaner ($92/96$ informative pairs, $p=5.1\times10^{-17}$): the buyback's defining purpose---re-acquiring previously liquidated collateral---is realized robustly.

\paragraph{The re-leverage guard is what makes buyback safe.}
The benign buyback result above depends critically on the health-factor floor. We isolate this by replaying the same real-window batch with the floor removed (reborrow allowed up to the LLTV capacity, the naive design). Without the floor, each recovery buy pushes the post-buy health factor down to $\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}\approx0.98$, re-arming the trigger: average sell events per run rise from $1.9$ (no buyback) to $3.0$ (unguarded buyback), and buyback then \emph{increases} mean borrower loss, with the one-sided Wilcoxon now significant in the opposite direction ($p\approx7\times10^{-8}$ that unguarded buyback increases loss). Reinstating the floor at $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}=1.20$ (with a $5\%$ spread requirement) returns sell intensity to $1.9$---i.e.\ buyback induces essentially no extra liquidation---and recovers the positive benefit reported above. Lot-matching and positive spread alone are therefore \emph{not} sufficient to prevent ping-pong; the reborrow cap is the operative safeguard.

\paragraph{Robustness across stress regimes.}
Because the per-run buyback benefit on real windows is modest, we confirm its direction on an independent, more adversarial regime: synthetic deep-crash paths (seeded drawdowns of $18$--$45\%$ with partial rebounds). Table~\ref{tab:stress_regime_comparison} contrasts the two. On crash paths the guarded buyback helps far more---mean loss reduction $\$1{,}090$ with buyback winning $77/77$ informative pairs ($p=1.2\times10^{-14}$)---because deep drawdowns followed by rebounds are exactly where re-acquiring collateral pays off. In both regimes buyback restores strictly more collateral (higher $\rho$), induces no extra sells, and produces zero bad debt. The benefit is thus small in calm sampled windows and large in crashes, but one-directional throughout.

\subsection{Experimental takeaway}
The evidence supports a clear, layered conclusion. (i) Target-HF soft liquidation robustly outperforms fixed-close-factor liquidation on borrower loss while keeping the position solvent and eliminating protocol bad debt, across both the real-window batch and the historical sweep. (ii) The buyback restoration leg is only safe under the health-factor-floor re-leverage guard: unguarded, it induces ping-pong re-liquidation and harms the borrower; guarded, it is a small, statistically significant, and strictly non-harmful improvement that lets the borrower recover rebound upside. (iii) Bad-debt feasibility, which earlier drafts found unattainable, holds for every swept candidate once positions start healthy and target-HF sizing is used. The mechanism is thus best viewed as a transparent control-and-accounting framework whose central deleveraging primitive (target-HF) carries a robust advantage, with buyback as a governance-optional, safety-gated restoration leg.

\section{Related Work and Positioning}
Hard-threshold liquidation systems prioritize simplicity and fast insolvency response but can induce cliff liquidation and borrower discontinuities. Soft-liquidation AMM approaches distribute liquidation over ranges via continuous trading curves. Our approach is closer to an endogenous control policy: it keeps staged liquidation intuition while preserving explicit event accounting and deterministic trigger semantics.

From a protocol comparison angle, Aave/Morpho-style liquidations emphasize fast external liquidator competition with target-HF partial-liquidation sizing but no explicit buyback restoration leg \cite{aave-risk-params,aave-liquidations}. As noted by recent DAO analysis \cite{aave-v4-spoke-revenue}, tuning Target-HF engines in a single-directional setup forces a strict trade-off between borrower UX and liquidation reliability. Our two-sided approach adds a recovery side to this design: positive-spread, reborrow-funded buybacks repair borrower collateral during rebounds. We find the recovery side is value-accretive (and not merely neutral) only when its reborrow is bounded by a health-factor floor; this re-leverage guard, rather than the buyback itself, is the load-bearing design element. Meanwhile, Curve crvUSD emphasizes continuous band migration and soft-liquidation with AMM-mediated execution \cite{curve-crvusd,curve-llamma,egorov2023crvusd}.

Empirical evidence on liquidation dynamics and cascading effects in DeFi further motivates tail-aware evaluation: prior work documents liquidation concentration, fragility around collateral boundaries, and protocol-level design externalities \cite{qin2021liquidations,perez2021knifeedge,gudgeon2020loanable}.

From a mechanism-design view, LLTV/LTV execution with target-HF sizing can be interpreted as a bounded control over collateral exposure. This offers an engineering advantage for protocol operators who require transparent stress testing and parameter governance.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Relative to hard-threshold liquidations, endogenous partial liquidation provides smoother deleveraging and explicit recovery semantics. Relative to AMM-coupled soft liquidation systems, this design emphasizes deterministic trigger rules and separated accounting of inventory and execution logic. The contribution here is mechanism structure and operational clarity, not a claim of universal dominance.

\section{Limitations and Evaluation Roadmap}
The current results should be interpreted with several caveats. Fixed dynamic-policy coefficients can underperform under regime shifts in volatility; execution quality is abstracted from explicit slippage and latency microstructure; and strategic adversarial behavior around trigger boundaries is not yet endogenized. The favorable bad-debt and borrower-loss results are conditional on positions starting healthy ($\mathrm{CR}_0=1.30$) so that target-HF sizing has room to act; positions that are already underwater at observation time cannot be repaired by partial liquidation and remain a residual bad-debt source. The borrower-loss metric is an opportunity cost relative to passive holding, so the measured buyback benefit reflects recovered rebound upside and is modest in magnitude; a borrower who does not value collateral re-exposure would weigh the buyback differently. Discrete-time replay can also amplify short-lived bad-debt peaks relative to finer-grained execution. Parameter governance for live positions is an open design problem, since safe migration across policy updates requires formally specified transition rules. Future work should prioritize volatility-adaptive coefficient updates, constrained or Pareto policy optimization, and adversarial stress tests with market-impact-aware execution.

\paragraph{Evaluation roadmap.}
A complete empirical evaluation should cross LLTV levels, close-factor slope and bounds, trigger hysteresis (buyback risk-guard bandwidth), market regime class, recovery policy, and redemption toggles. The relevant outputs are restoration--profit frontiers, event turnover and slippage sensitivity, tail bad-debt statistics, and depeg half-life proxies. This design makes risk-management trade-offs directly measurable.

\section{Conclusion}
Target-HF LLTV/LTV soft liquidation with guarded buyback provides a practical, auditable risk-control primitive for volatile lending markets. Across a real-window stress batch and rolling historical windows, target-HF partial liquidation robustly outperforms fixed-close-factor liquidation on borrower loss while keeping positions solvent and incurring zero bad debt. The buyback restoration leg is beneficial only when its reborrow is bounded by a health-factor floor: without the floor it induces ping-pong re-liquidation and harms the borrower, whereas with it buyback becomes a safe, statistically significant, strictly non-harmful improvement. The re-leverage guard is thus an essential---and, to our knowledge, previously unaddressed---ingredient of any two-sided liquidation-restoration design. These findings, regenerated end-to-end by the released scripts, establish a reproducible baseline for subsequent constrained calibration and external validation on broader market datasets.

\label{sec:repro}

\section{Event-Engine Pseudocode}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialize loan state $C_0=D\,\mathrm{CR}_0/P_0$, $U_0=0$, $\Pi_0=0$, and policy parameters $(\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{HF}^{\star},b,\eta,\delta,\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}},s)$.
  \item For each new price $P_t$, compute $\mathrm{LTV}_t$ and evaluate liquidation eligibility first:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$ (require $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}$ for feasibility), compute target repayment $\Delta D_t^{\star}=D_t(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)/(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b))$, clip by debt/collateral constraints,
    and execute $q_t^{\text{sell}}=(1+b)\Delta D_t/P_t$.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Evaluate buyback risk-guard condition:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$, match an open sell lot with $P_i^{\text{sell}}>(1+s)P_t$ (partial allowed), and reborrow at most $B_t^{\star}=\max(0,(\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t-\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}D_t)/(\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}-\mathrm{LT}))$ so the post-buy health factor stays $\ge\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$.
  \end{itemize}
  \item For terminal states, allow protective buyback even when incremental trade P\&L is negative.
  \item Update debt and inventories, then recompute derived risk metrics (HF, LTV, CR, bad debt).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{abstract}
This paper proposes a two-sided, band-based collateral-restoration mechanism for collateralized lending. As the market falls, target-HF partial liquidations sell collateral and record an endogenous price band; as the market rebounds, the mechanism buys collateral back at the band bottom, funded by reborrowing. Our central contribution concerns \emph{how much} to buy back: we show that the recovery quantity must not be a one-to-one match to the previously sold lots---such a rule re-levers the position and triggers ping-pong re-liquidation that harms the borrower---but should instead be governed by a solvency constraint, a health-factor floor on the reborrow that bounds re-acquisition at the band bottom. We measure the contribution against the right baseline: not legacy fixed-close-factor liquidation (which target-HF Pareto-dominates, but that is Aave's result), but Aave v4's modern \emph{sell-only} target-HF engine. The only difference is the buyback leg, so the comparison isolates its marginal value. We are candid that this value is modest in dollars and conditional---the leg is dormant when there is no drawdown--rebound cycle (no harm), and its USD benefit is a horizon-dependent bet---but it is a safe, near-free option: against the Aave-v4 baseline it restores strictly more collateral (an accounting guarantee), adds no bad debt, and induces almost no extra liquidation. Because its value depends on borrower preferences (numeraire, horizon, asset conviction), we frame it as a \emph{per-borrower opt-in module}---restoration-seeking long-horizon holders elect it, USD-PnL borrowers decline it---which heterogeneous collateralized borrowers self-select without externalizing any risk onto the pool, and which the protocol then executes automatically on their behalf (set-and-forget) through permissionless keepers. We validate the fixed, tuned policy out of sample on $15$ assets over 2019--2024 (spanning the 2022 multi-leg crashes). There we find ``zero bad debt'' is not unconditional but a point on a sell-side governance frontier (an early trigger drives worst-case bad debt to a few hundred USD at a modest borrower-loss cost), and that the effective buy-side principle is \emph{stress-tested sizing}---sizing each buy to survive a further price drop---which keeps the restoration leg safe and beneficial on adversarial paths, whereas confirmed-upturn timing gates add little. An exact accounting identity makes the guarded buyback's value precise and numeraire-dependent: it restores strictly more collateral on \emph{every} path ($\Delta\rho=\sum_i q_i/C_0>0$, unconditional), while its USD outcome equals $\sum_i q_i(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})$---a basket of band-bottom leveraged-long positions whose sign depends on the terminal price relative to the buy prices. The leg is thus unconditionally favorable for a borrower who values the collateral asset or holds a long-horizon recovery conviction, and a directional bet for one marking to USD over a fixed horizon. Empirically, across rolling historical windows and synthetic deep-crash paths, the guarded leg never induces extra liquidation and incurs zero bad debt; its USD benefit is small and dormant on calm windows (it acts only over a full drawdown--rebound cycle) and large in crashes, tracing a restoration-vs-re-leverage trade-off set explicitly by the floor. We report effect sizes with attention to window dependence---the rolling windows overlap, so their paired $p$-values are descriptive rather than independent evidence---and provide a fully reproducible protocol that regenerates every result.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Liquidation is central to solvency in overcollateralized lending. Most deployed systems are built around one or a small number of hard CR thresholds, where falling below the threshold triggers immediate liquidation pressure. In highly volatile markets, this can create cliff effects: borrowers experience abrupt collateral loss, while protocols face concentrated execution risk.

This paper proposes \emph{state-contingent two-sided partial liquidation}, a rule-based alternative that replaces fixed-quantity bucket rules with state-driven actions. As collateral value declines, liquidation is triggered by LTV/LLTV breaches and sized by a target-HF repair requirement; when risk normalizes, collateral is bought back at the band bottom, funded through constrained reborrowing under a health-factor floor. The objective is not to eliminate liquidation, but to transform it from a discontinuous event into a staged control policy with auditable behavior.

Hard-threshold lending liquidations are widely documented in production protocols such as Aave, where positions are monitored by health-factor style constraints and liquidators repay debt for discounted collateral claims \cite{aave-liquidations}. Target-HF partial-liquidation sizing---where debt repayment is computed to restore the borrower's health factor to a governance-set target rather than applying a fixed close factor---has been formally adopted in Aave v4's liquidation engine \cite{aave-v4-liquidations} and is under active exploration in Morpho-style designs. Recent analysis of Aave v4 configurations highlights a fundamental trade-off in single-directional Target-HF systems: parameters must either favor borrower protection (resulting in low surplus and potential liveness risks during stress) or liquidation efficiency (providing high protocol revenue and execution reliability, but inflicting maximum penalties on undercollateralized borrowers) \cite{aave-v4-spoke-revenue}. Furthermore, these implementations generally stop at sell-side deleveraging and do not include an endogenous protocol buyback leg for collateral restoration. In contrast, Curve's crvUSD design introduces soft-liquidation ranges through LLAMMA, where collateral is gradually converted across price bands rather than liquidated in one step \cite{curve-crvusd,curve-llamma}.

This paper takes target-HF sell-side sizing as a shared starting point (it is Aave v4's design, and we use it only as the deleveraging baseline) and contributes the \emph{band-based restoration} dimension: a two-sided rule that sells at the top of an endogenous price band and buys collateral back at its bottom on rebounds, reborrow-funded. The crux is the recovery \emph{quantity}. We show that matching the buyback one-to-one to the previously sold lots is the wrong rule: because a reborrow-funded buy lowers the health factor, lot-matched recovery re-levers the position back toward the trigger and induces ping-pong re-liquidation that harms the borrower. The quantity should instead be governed by solvency---a health-factor floor on the reborrow---and allocated across the band rather than per lot. Specifically, the study contributes: (i) the band sell-high/buy-low restoration mechanism with a \emph{solvency-governed, non-lot-matched} recovery quantity, and the \emph{health-factor-floor re-leverage guard} $B_t^{\star}$ that provably preserves a post-buy solvency buffer and eliminates ping-pong; (ii) the feasibility condition $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}$ under which target-HF repair is well posed, with a baseline confirmation that target-HF dominates fixed-close-factor liquidation at zero bad debt; (iii) a transparent accounting decomposition of debt reduction, reserve accumulation, and collateral restoration for pathwise stress diagnostics; and (iv) a fully reproducible event-level pipeline---including the table- and statistics-generation scripts---that supports paired policy comparison under identical paths.

\section{Model Setup}
Consider a loan with debt $D$ (USD), collateral amount $C$ in a generic risky asset, initial asset price $P_0$, and initial collateral ratio $\mathrm{CR}_0>1$.
Initial collateral amount is
\[
C_0 = \frac{D\,\mathrm{CR}_0}{P_0}.
\]

Let LLTV denote the liquidation LTV threshold. For a single-collateral position with debt $D_t$ and collateral value $C_tP_t$, define
\[
\mathrm{LTV}_t = \frac{D_t}{C_tP_t}, \qquad
\mathrm{HF}_t = \frac{C_tP_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t}=\frac{\mathrm{LT}}{\mathrm{LTV}_t}.
\]
Liquidation eligibility is state-defined: a sell action is considered when $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge \mathrm{LLTV}$ (equivalently when HF enters the liquidation region implied by LT/LLTV calibration).

\paragraph{Endogenous partial liquidation via LLTV/LTV.}
In the current implementation, partial liquidation is sized to restore the position toward a target health factor $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}$, rather than using a fixed liquidation amount. When $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge \mathrm{LLTV}$, the engine computes the debt reduction required to move the post-trade state to the target region. With liquidation bonus $b\ge0$, sell quantity is
\[
q_t^{\text{sell}}=\frac{(1+b)\Delta D_t}{P_t},
\]
and the target-HF condition is
\[
\mathrm{HF}^{\star}=\frac{\left(C_t-\frac{(1+b)\Delta D_t}{P_t}\right)P_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t-\Delta D_t}.
\]
Solving gives the target repayment proposal
\[
\Delta D_t^{\star}=\frac{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}D_t-\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t}{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b)},
\]
which is then clipped by debt and available collateral constraints. Using $\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t=\mathrm{HF}_t D_t$, the proposal admits the equivalent, more interpretable form
\[
\Delta D_t^{\star}=\frac{D_t\,(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)}{\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b)},
\]
so the trade size scales with the health-factor gap $(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)$. The repair is well posed only when both factors are positive: the numerator requires $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{HF}_t$ (the position is below target), and at the trigger boundary $\mathrm{LTV}_t=\mathrm{LLTV}$ this becomes the design feasibility condition
\[
\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\frac{\mathrm{LT}}{\mathrm{LLTV}},
\]
while the denominator requires $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}(1+b)$. If these fail, the target is infeasible and the engine falls back to full repayment. (For example, $\mathrm{LLTV}=0.85$, $\mathrm{LT}=0.83$ gives $\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}=0.976$, so any $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}\ge 1$ is feasible; conversely $\mathrm{LLTV}=0.72$ would require $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>1.153$, and a nominal $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}=1.05$ would never fire.)

\paragraph{Trigger placement (LLTV vs.\ LT).} The trigger $\mathrm{LLTV}$ and the threshold $\mathrm{LT}$ in the health factor are distinct knobs, and their order sets how early the mechanism acts. With $\mathrm{LLTV}<\mathrm{LT}$ the first sell fires while $\mathrm{HF}>1$ (genuinely early/pre-emptive deleveraging); with $\mathrm{LLTV}>\mathrm{LT}$---as in our swept optimum $\mathrm{LLTV}=0.85>\mathrm{LT}=0.83$, where the trigger sits at $\mathrm{HF}=\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}=0.976$---the mechanism tolerates a small insolvency margin before acting. The sweep favors the later trigger because the opportunity-cost objective (below) rewards selling less and later, and under the smooth discrete price paths used here this incurs no bad debt. This is a deliberate but path-dependent choice: under gap moves a late trigger raises insolvency risk, so a conservative deployment would set $\mathrm{LLTV}\le\mathrm{LT}$ to keep an early-action safety margin. We treat $\mathrm{LLTV}$ relative to $\mathrm{LT}$ as a governance dial between borrower opportunity cost and gap-risk protection, not as a tuned constant.

If price continues to fall after the first repair trade, the position can re-enter the liquidation region and trigger a second partial liquidation. The sequence of discrete partial liquidations naturally generates an \emph{endogenous price band}: a recorded grid of sell levels $\{P_{t_1}^{\text{sell}},P_{t_2}^{\text{sell}},\dots\}$ with associated quantities $\{q_{t_1}^{\text{sell}},q_{t_2}^{\text{sell}},\dots\}$, accumulated without any pre-specified band geometry. The core mechanism we propose operates on this band in two directions: it \emph{sells at the band top} (the high prices at which liquidations occurred as the market fell) and \emph{buys back at the band bottom} (when the market returns to prices below those sell levels). The recorded grid is the path-dependent memory that makes a safe rebound unwind possible.

A central design question is the recovery \emph{quantity}. A fundamental risk is ``ping-pong liquidation'', where a debt-funded buyback immediately degrades the health factor and triggers a secondary liquidation: because a reborrow-funded buyback adds equal USD amounts of collateral and debt, it necessarily \emph{lowers} the health factor. We argue---and show empirically (\S\ref{sec:results})---that the recovery quantity should therefore \emph{not} be a one-to-one match to the sold lots (a naive ``buy back what you sold'' rule re-levers the position straight back toward the trigger). Instead, the quantity is governed by a solvency constraint: a health-factor floor on the reborrow (\S\ref{par:hffloor}) that bounds how much can be re-acquired at the band bottom, consumed across the open grid from the band top down. The price grid sets \emph{when and where} to buy (positive spread below a recorded sell level); the solvency floor sets \emph{how much}.

Buyback is enabled under a risk-guard condition, implemented as
\[
\mathrm{LTV}_t\le \mathrm{LLTV}+\delta,
\]
where $\delta\ge0$ is a buyback risk-guard bandwidth. The buy triggers when the price sits below the band by at least a spread $s\ge0$, i.e.\ when some open grid level satisfies $P_i^{\text{sell}} > (1+s)\,P_t$, and the total recovered inventory is capped by a buyback ratio $\eta\in(0,1]$ via $Q_t^{\text{bought,total}}\le\eta\,Q_t^{\text{sold,total}}$. The executed quantity is \emph{not} tied to any single lot; it is the solvency-bounded amount below, allocated across the eligible grid from the band top (highest sell price) down.

\paragraph{Health-factor-floor re-leverage guard.}
\label{par:hffloor}
The operative constraint is on \emph{how much} can be reborrowed. A naive rule reborrows up to the LLTV capacity, $\,C_tP_t\,\mathrm{LLTV}-D_t$, which drives the post-buy LTV straight back to LLTV and re-arms the trigger. Instead, we cap the reborrow so that the post-buy health factor stays above a floor $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}>\mathrm{LT}$. Since a buyback of USD value $x$ sets $C\mapsto C+x/P_t$ and $D\mapsto D+x$, the post-buy health factor $\mathrm{HF}=\mathrm{LT}(C_tP_t+x)/(D_t+x)\ge\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$ holds iff
\[
x \le B_t^{\star}=\max\!\left(0,\ \frac{\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t-\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}D_t}{\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}-\mathrm{LT}}\right),
\]
so the recovery quantity is $q_t^{\text{buy}}=\min\!\big(B_t^{\star}/P_t,\ \eta Q_t^{\text{sold,total}}-Q_t^{\text{bought,total}},\ \text{open grid inventory}\big)$. This guarantees a solvency buffer after every recovery buy and is what eliminates ping-pong re-liquidation; a lot-matched quantity with positive spread alone does not (\S\ref{sec:results}). The floor also exposes the central trade-off: a lower $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$ permits larger restoration but admits more re-leverage, while a higher floor is safer but recovers less.

\paragraph{Stress-tested sizing for multi-leg crashes.} The floor $B_t^{\star}$ keeps post-buy HF$\,\ge\,$floor at the \emph{current} price, which suffices when a buy is followed by recovery but not when the market makes a further leg down: a buy made mid-crash re-levers the position and can re-arm the trigger on the next decline. We therefore size against a \emph{stressed} price $P_t(1-d)$, requiring post-buy HF$\,\ge 1$ even after a further drop of fraction $d$. This is the same identity with $\mathrm{LT}\mapsto\mathrm{LT}(1-d)$ and floor $1$:
\[
B_t^{\star,\text{stress}}=\max\!\left(0,\ \frac{\mathrm{LT}(1-d)\,C_tP_t-D_t}{1-\mathrm{LT}(1-d)}\right),
\]
and we take $q_t^{\text{buy}}\le B_t^{\star,\text{stress}}/P_t$ in addition to $B_t^{\star}$. Out of sample this is the single most effective buy-side principle (\S\ref{sec:results}). We also support an optional ``confirmed-upturn'' timing gate---buy only after the price has bounced off a recent local low---but find it adds little, because one cannot reliably distinguish a true bottom from a dead-cat bounce ex ante: conservative \emph{sizing} dominates clever \emph{timing}.

Relative to a stricter guard $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}-\delta$, using $\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$ is intended to activate restoration earlier during rebounds and to reduce missed recovery opportunities in choppy regimes. This choice introduces an overlap interval $[\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta]$ where sell and buy conditions can both hold in principle. In implementation, conflict is resolved deterministically by execution order: sell logic is evaluated first, and buyback is checked only on the post-sell state within the same step.

\section{Mechanism and Accounting}
The mechanism maintains an execution state and inventory record per position. A sell event is triggered by an LLTV breach ($\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$); its size is computed online from the target-HF repair requirement, debt, and price, and the executed level/quantity are appended to the price band. A buy event is governed by a risk guard ($\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$) together with the band-bottom condition (the price lies below a recorded sell level by the spread $s$); its quantity is the solvency-bounded amount $q_t^{\text{buy}}$ of \S2, allocated across the open band from the top down rather than matched to a single lot. In terminal states, the policy may allow loss-making buyback during sufficiently strong recovery; this preserves explicit restoration semantics rather than purely myopic profit maximization.

Operationally, each sell event carries a liquidation bonus. With gross sell proceeds $q_t^{\text{sell}}P_t=(1+b)\Delta D_t$ and debt reduction $\Delta D_t$, the protocol reserve increment is $\Delta U_t=q_t^{\text{sell}}P_t-\Delta D_t=b\,\Delta D_t$---exactly the bonus. This reserve accrues to the protocol as first-loss capital and is \emph{not} the source of buyback funding: recovery buys are financed by reborrowing (debt re-expansion), under the health-factor floor of \S2, so the restoration leg and the reserve are accounted separately and the reserve is never drawn down by buybacks.

The borrower holds the position $(C_t,D_t)$; the protocol separately holds reserve $U_t$. We characterize the buyback leg by what it adds to the borrower's terminal outcome relative to the same policy with buyback disabled (no-buyback). Under the health-factor floor the buyback induces no additional liquidation (\S\ref{sec:results}), so the two policies execute identical sells and differ only in the recovery buys $\{(q_i,P_i^{\text{buy}})\}$. A buy adds $q_i$ collateral and $q_iP_i^{\text{buy}}$ reborrowed debt, so the borrower's terminal equity gain from buyback, marked at the terminal price $P_T$, is exactly
\[
\Delta\mathrm{Eq}_T \;=\; \underbrace{\Big(\textstyle\sum_i q_i\Big)P_T}_{\text{extra collateral}} \;-\; \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_i q_iP_i^{\text{buy}}}_{\text{extra debt}} \;=\; \sum_i q_i\,(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}}),
\]
while the extra collateral retained, in units, is $\Delta C_T=\sum_i q_i>0$.

\paragraph{Two numeraires.} These two identities make the buyback's value precise and, importantly, \emph{numeraire-dependent}.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Collateral numeraire.} The restoration ratio gain $\Delta\rho=\Delta C_T/C_0=\sum_i q_i/C_0>0$ is \emph{unconditional}: a buyback only ever adds collateral, so it always restores strictly more of the underlying asset, on every path.
\item \textbf{USD numeraire.} The fiat gain $\Delta\mathrm{Eq}_T=\sum_i q_i(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})$ is a basket of long-collateral positions opened at the band bottom: it is positive exactly when the terminal price exceeds the buy prices. The positive-spread guard ensures each buy is below its matched sell ($P_i^{\text{buy}}<P_i^{\text{sell}}$), but not that $P_T>P_i^{\text{buy}}$; if price falls further after a buy, that leg shows a fiat loss.
\end{itemize}
The buyback is therefore best understood as a band-bottom, leveraged-long restoration of the collateral asset. For a borrower whose objective is to retain the asset---or who holds a long-horizon conviction that it eventually recovers above the band-bottom buy prices---the recovery is favorable and, in fiat, eventually profitable. For a borrower marking to USD over a fixed, possibly short horizon, the same leg is a directional bet whose sign depends on the terminal price. Because the value of the leg depends on the borrower's preferences---numeraire (asset vs.\ USD), horizon, and conviction in the collateral---rather than being uniformly positive, the natural design is to make it a \emph{per-borrower opt-in module} rather than a forced policy: restoration-seeking, long-horizon holders elect it; USD-PnL, short-horizon borrowers decline it. This is exactly why ``optional'' is a feature, not a hedge: the mechanism offers a menu and lets heterogeneous collateralized borrowers self-select, and in both cases the protocol bears no incremental risk---the floor caps re-leverage and (\S\ref{sec:results}) bad debt stays zero, so enabling the option for some borrowers never externalizes risk onto the pool or onto borrowers who opt out.

\paragraph{Friction: why the floor is economic, not merely prudential.} The clean identities above hold \emph{because the floor prevents induced re-liquidation}, so the two policies share the same sells. This is the crux, because liquidation is not free: each sell of $q^{\text{sell}}=(1+b)\Delta D/P$ to retire $\Delta D$ of debt costs the borrower the bonus $b\,\Delta D$ in equity (plus execution slippage $\sigma$), permanently and independently of the terminal price. A buyback that re-levers the position back toward the trigger therefore does not merely ``churn''; every extra liquidation cycle it provokes burns another $b\,\Delta D$ (and slippage). Writing $\Delta\widetilde D$ for the additional debt liquidated by buyback-induced re-leverage, the borrower's true buyback value is
\[
\Delta\mathrm{Eq}_T \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_i q_i\,(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})}_{\text{band-bottom leveraged long}} \;-\; \underbrace{(b+\sigma)\,\Delta\widetilde D}_{\text{friction burned by ping-pong}}.
\]
Without the floor the second term grows with each re-liquidation and can dominate, so the leg degenerates into a friction-bleeding ``buy-and-hold-through-anything'' leveraged long that is net-negative \emph{even in collateral terms} once the bonus paid to run the cycle is counted. The floor's role is thus economic: by driving $\Delta\widetilde D\to 0$ it makes the restoration friction-efficient---restoring collateral while paying the liquidation bonus as few times as possible. Our simulations charge the bonus $b$ (so the ping-pong drag is already visible as worse borrower outcomes under the unguarded rule, \S\ref{sec:results}) but abstract from slippage $\sigma$; with realistic slippage the case for the floor is only stronger.

\paragraph{Permissionless, system-executed two sides.} Crucially, the opt-in is \emph{set-and-forget}: the borrower elects the option once (and its parameters---the floor $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$ and stress buffer $d$), and the protocol thereafter executes the restoration \emph{on the borrower's behalf}, without any active management by the borrower. Both legs are permissionless and deterministic-by-rule, so keepers compete to execute them: one side races to deleverage on the way down, the other to buy back at the band bottom on the way up, each rewarded by the positive grid spread. The borrower neither monitors the market nor times trades; the system does. Realized protocol round-trip P\&L over completed sell--buy cycles is $\Pi=\sum_k q_k(P_k^{\text{sell}}-P_k^{\text{buy}})$, positive by the positive-spread guard on each matched cycle; the borrower-side fiat outcome above is a separate ledger. The mechanism thus reallocates liquidation risk over time instead of concentrating it at a single boundary, and does so as an automated, rule-driven service rather than a manual strategy.

\section{Simulation Framework and Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}
This section reports a unified, fully reproducible evaluation pipeline for the implemented mechanism. The objective is mechanism validation under path uncertainty: we evaluate borrower outcomes, solvency/tail risk, and execution behavior under both a real-window stress batch and rolling historical windows. All tables and statistics in this section are regenerated from the run outputs by a single script (\S\ref{sec:repro}).

\subsection{Experimental design and policy set}
All experiments compare three policies under matched initial conditions and matched price paths:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{traditional\_fixed\_cf}: fixed close-factor liquidation---a \emph{legacy} baseline included only for context, since production protocols have moved past it.
  \item \texttt{target\_hf\_no\_buyback}: sell-only target-HF partial liquidation. This is our proxy for the \emph{real modern baseline}, Aave v4's single-directional target-HF engine, and is the policy our contribution must beat; the only difference from the proposal is the absence of the buyback leg.
  \item proposed target-HF with buyback (scenario key \texttt{baseline\_dynamic}).
\end{itemize}
Accordingly, the headline question is not whether target-HF beats fixed-CF (it does, but that is Aave's result), but the \emph{marginal value of the guarded buyback leg over the Aave-v4-style baseline}---a deliberately demanding comparison, since the baseline already deleverages well and incurs little bad debt. Because the two policies share an identical sell side and differ only in the buyback, this isolates the leg's effect cleanly.
For the dynamic policy, buyback is band-based, positive-spread, reborrow-funded execution under the health-factor-floor re-leverage guard of \S2. Specifically, each buy fires at the band bottom (price below a recorded sell level by the spread $s$), and its quantity respects the reborrow cap $B_t^{\star}$ that keeps the post-buy health factor above $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$---deliberately not a one-to-one match to any sold lot. This keeps restoration behavior auditable, avoids reserve-funded shortcuts, and prevents the re-leverage from re-arming the liquidation trigger. Each loan is initialized at a controlled $\mathrm{CR}_0$ at its own start price ($C_0=D\,\mathrm{CR}_0/P_0$), so that policies are compared on identically healthy starting positions and stress is driven by the price path rather than by initial-condition heterogeneity.

\subsection{State variables, metrics, and implementation alignment}
At each step, the simulator records
\[
\mathrm{HF}_t = \frac{C_tP_t\,\mathrm{LT}}{D_t},\qquad
\mathrm{LTV}_t = \frac{D_t}{C_tP_t},\qquad
\mathrm{CR}_t = \frac{C_tP_t}{D_t}.
\]
Sell triggers are evaluated at $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$ and buyback guard at $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$. Event logs include debt repayment, collateral sold/bought, reserve changes, and trigger-level snapshots.

Primary evaluation metrics are borrower final loss, restoration ratio $\rho=C_T/C_0$, peak bad debt $\max_t\max(0,D_t-C_tP_t)$, event turnover (sell/buy intensity), and minimum health factor. Borrower final loss is measured as an \emph{opportunity cost relative to passive holding}, $\max(0,\,C_0P_T-D_0-(C_TP_T-D_T))$: it captures how much worse the borrower is, marked at the terminal price $P_T$, than if the initial collateral had simply been held. Under this metric the value of buyback is precise---by re-acquiring previously sold collateral, it lets the borrower participate in rebounds and reduces missed upside. We report both central tendency and tail-sensitive statistics to avoid selecting policies that only win in average-case regimes.

Key configuration dimensions are as follows: loan notional and initial CR define initial position scale; LLTV and target-HF levels control trigger and repair intensity; close-factor/liquidation-bonus sets determine liquidation aggressiveness; buyback ratio and bandwidth determine recovery-side activation; and random-seed/path-window settings govern stress-batch and historical-window sampling. Exact values are scenario-dependent and are reported together with each result table/sweep output.

\subsection{Real-window stress-batch evidence}
We use a $600$-run stress batch formed by $200$ distinct windows sampled from the real normalized ETH price series crossed with three initial collateralizations $\mathrm{CR}_0\in\{1.25,1.35,1.50\}$ (all above the trigger $1/\mathrm{LLTV}$), the loan being initialized at $\mathrm{CR}_0$ at its window start price. The multiple CR levels increase the number of \emph{informative} (non-tie) paired comparisons and hence statistical power. (Under initial-CR normalization a uniform price shock cancels from the CR path, so stress is induced by genuine sampled drawdown/rebound dynamics rather than by synthetic multiplicative shocks.)

Two patterns are clear. First, both target-HF policies dominate the fixed-close-factor baseline on the central borrower outcome: median borrower loss falls from $2{,}753$ USD (fixed CF) to $0$ USD (target-HF, i.e.\ more than half of runs incur no opportunity-cost loss), the minimum health factor stays above $1$ ($1.027$ vs.\ $0.98$ for fixed CF, which dips into the insolvency region), collateral restoration $\rho$ is higher ($0.594$ vs.\ $0.584$), and worst-case bad debt is $0$ USD for target-HF against $27{,}624$ USD for fixed CF. Target-HF thus deleverages more smoothly while eliminating the protocol's bad-debt exposure. Second, adding the guarded buyback raises restoration (mean $\rho$ from $0.5922$ to $0.5944$) and slightly lowers mean borrower loss ($13{,}303\to13{,}255$ USD). Here we are careful about what the statistics do and do not show. The restoration gain is guaranteed by the accounting identity of \S3---a buyback only ever adds collateral, so $\Delta\rho>0$ on every path---and the data bear this out on $92/96$ informative windows; this is the robust, numeraire-clean statement of the buyback's value. The USD-loss reduction, by contrast, equals $\sum_i q_i(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})$ and is therefore a directional bet on the terminal price: it is positive only when the price ends above the band-bottom buy prices. It is also \emph{dormant} on most windows---the buyback acts only over a full drawdown--rebound cycle, so short or trendless windows simply see no buys (no help, but no harm)---and the apparent per-run reduction (e.g.\ $\$47.6$ over the active windows) rests on a small number of activations. We deliberately do not lean on the rolling-window Wilcoxon $p$-values for this leg: the $200$ windows are overlapping draws from a single price series, so the paired differences are autocorrelated and such $p$-values overstate independent significance (under non-overlapping windows across ETH and BTC, activations are too sparse to reach significance on calm data; the USD benefit is material only in deep-crash--strong-rebound regimes, \S\ref{sec:results} below). The honest summary is that the guarded buyback restores collateral unconditionally and safely, and improves the USD outcome conditionally---substantially in crashes, negligibly to slightly on calm paths, never at the cost of extra liquidation or bad debt.

\paragraph{The re-leverage guard is what makes buyback safe.}
The benign buyback result above depends critically on the health-factor floor. We isolate this by replaying the same real-window batch with the floor removed (reborrow allowed up to the LLTV capacity, the naive design). Without the floor, each recovery buy pushes the post-buy health factor down to $\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}\approx0.98$, re-arming the trigger: average sell events per run rise from $1.9$ (no buyback) to $3.0$ (unguarded buyback), and buyback then \emph{increases} mean borrower loss, with the one-sided Wilcoxon now significant in the opposite direction ($p\approx7\times10^{-8}$ that unguarded buyback increases loss). Reinstating the floor at $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}=1.20$ (with a $5\%$ spread requirement) returns sell intensity to $1.9$---i.e.\ buyback induces essentially no extra liquidation---and recovers the positive benefit reported above. Lot-matching and positive spread alone are therefore \emph{not} sufficient to prevent ping-pong; the reborrow cap is the operative safeguard.

\paragraph{Robustness across stress regimes.}
Because the per-run buyback benefit on real windows is modest, we confirm its direction on an independent, more adversarial regime: synthetic deep-crash paths (seeded drawdowns of $18$--$45\%$ with partial rebounds). Table~\ref{tab:stress_regime_comparison} contrasts the two. On crash paths the guarded buyback helps far more---mean loss reduction $\$1{,}090$ with buyback winning $77/77$ informative pairs ($p=1.2\times10^{-14}$)---because deep drawdowns followed by rebounds are exactly where re-acquiring collateral pays off. In both regimes buyback restores strictly more collateral (higher $\rho$), induces no extra sells, and produces zero bad debt. The benefit is thus small in calm sampled windows and large in crashes, but one-directional throughout.

\paragraph{The health-factor floor as a governance dial.}
The floor $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$ is the single parameter that trades restoration against re-leverage, and it exposes a clean frontier (Table~\ref{tab:hf_floor_frontier}, buyback ratio $\eta=1$ so the floor is the sole governor). As the floor is lowered, the buyback restores more collateral and reduces borrower loss more on average---dramatically so in crashes (e.g.\ $\$5{,}738$ at $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}=1.05$ vs $\$894$ at $1.20$)---but it harms a growing minority of borrowers and induces more re-liquidation (extra sells per run rising from $\approx0$ to $1.5$ at the naive borrow-to-LLTV extreme). Notably, \emph{bad debt remains zero at every floor}: the target-HF sell side keeps the protocol solvent, so the floor governs borrower-side restoration-vs-re-leverage, not protocol insolvency. Two operating points are natural: $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}=1.20$ is \emph{Pareto-safe}---it essentially never makes any borrower worse off ($3/600$ losses) while still significantly reducing loss and restoring collateral---and $\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}=1.10$ delivers a markedly larger net benefit ($\$137$ real, $\$3{,}264$ crash, $p<10^{-4}$) at the cost of harming a small minority ($23/600$). We adopt $1.20$ as the default and treat the dial as a governance choice between Pareto-safety and net-benefit maximization.

\subsection{Out-of-sample validation on multi-asset, multi-year data}
\label{sec:oos}
The results so far are tuned and evaluated on a single ETH series. To test generalization, we apply the \emph{fixed} swept candidate---without any re-tuning---to $15$ assets over 2019--2024 (BTC, ETH, LTC, BCH, ETC, LINK, XLM, EOS, ZEC, XTZ, ADA, SOL, DOT, AVAX, MATIC; daily Coinbase prices), on non-overlapping $270$-day windows ($95$ independent window-paths, clustered over three CR levels). This window spans the 2021 bull market and the 2022 multi-leg crashes (Terra/Luna, FTX), so it is genuinely adversarial.

Two findings are robust out of sample. First, the target-HF deleveraging baseline \emph{Pareto-dominates} fixed-CF: across the sell-side dial (Table~\ref{tab:oos_sell_frontier}) every $(\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{HF}^{\star})$ configuration has both lower worst-case bad debt and no-worse borrower loss than the fixed-CF baseline (mean loss $48{,}389$, worst bad debt $26{,}555$). Second, and importantly, ``zero bad debt'' is \emph{not} an unconditional property out of sample---it is a point on a governance frontier. With the loss-minimizing late trigger ($\mathrm{LLTV}=0.85$) the worst-window bad debt is $9{,}419$ USD; moving to an early, pre-emptive trigger ($\mathrm{LLTV}=0.70$, $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}=1.25$) drives it to $265$ USD (and the number of windows with any bad debt from $13$ to $2$), at a ${+}27\%$ borrower-loss cost. Earlier deleveraging buys solvency with borrower opportunity cost; the mechanism makes that trade-off an explicit dial rather than a hidden constant.

On the buy side, the out-of-sample data exposes a failure mode invisible in-sample: on multi-leg crashes a buy at the band bottom can be followed by a further decline, so even the floor-guarded buyback induces some extra liquidation (mean $+0.6$, worst $+6.3$ extra sells per window) and its collateral restoration is no longer unconditional ($26$ win / $22$ lose). The stress-tested sizing principle of \S\ref{par:hffloor} fixes this: sizing buys to survive a further $d$ drop cuts induced re-liquidation sharply and restores clean, significant restoration and USD benefit (Table~\ref{tab:oos_buy_principles}). At $d=0.25$ the buyback wins restoration in $24/35$ informative windows ($p=9\times10^{-4}$) and reduces USD loss on $17/19$ active windows ($p=4\times10^{-4}$), with worst extra sells more than halved. A confirmed-upturn timing gate adds little---conservative \emph{sizing} beats \emph{timing}, since a true bottom and a dead-cat bounce are indistinguishable ex ante. Residual bad debt is sell-side (the buy principles do not touch it) and the most extreme crashes (e.g.\ near-total collapses) exceed any fixed buffer; these are the limits of the parameterization, not of the accounting.

\subsection{Experimental takeaway}
The evidence supports a clear, layered conclusion, and the mechanism is best understood as two explicit governance dials over a target-HF baseline. (i) \textbf{Baseline}: target-HF sell-side sizing (Aave v4's design) Pareto-dominates fixed-CF out of sample---lower bad debt and no-worse borrower loss across the whole sell-side frontier. (ii) \textbf{Sell-side dial} ($\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{HF}^{\star}$): trades borrower opportunity cost against protocol solvency. ``Zero bad debt'' is not unconditional---it holds in-sample on smooth paths but, on adversarial multi-asset data, is a frontier choice: a pre-emptive early trigger drives worst-window bad debt to $265$ USD at a ${+}27\%$ loss cost, while the loss-minimizing late trigger leaves $9{,}419$. (iii) \textbf{Buy-side dial} (the restoration leg). Two of its properties are accounting guarantees: the floor caps re-leverage and the identity makes it restore strictly more collateral when the guard holds ($\Delta\rho>0$); the borrower's USD outcome $\sum_i q_i(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})$ is a band-bottom leveraged long, dormant on calm horizons and large in crash--rebound regimes. Out of sample the key buy principle is \emph{stress-tested sizing}: sizing to survive a further drop makes restoration and USD benefit clean and significant and slashes induced re-liquidation, whereas timing gates add little. (iv) Residuals are honest: extreme near-total collapses exceed any fixed buffer, and residual bad debt is sell-side. The contribution is thus a transparent, friction-efficient, two-sided control whose safety rests on accounting guarantees and whose loss/solvency/restoration trade-offs are explicit, governance-set dials---validated out of sample on $15$ assets across 2019--2024.

\section{Related Work and Positioning}
Hard-threshold liquidation systems prioritize simplicity and fast insolvency response but can induce cliff liquidation and borrower discontinuities. Soft-liquidation AMM approaches distribute liquidation over ranges via continuous trading curves. Our approach is closer to an endogenous control policy: it keeps staged liquidation intuition while preserving explicit event accounting and deterministic trigger semantics.

From a protocol comparison angle, Aave/Morpho-style liquidations emphasize fast external liquidator competition with target-HF partial-liquidation sizing but no explicit buyback restoration leg \cite{aave-risk-params,aave-liquidations}. As noted by recent DAO analysis \cite{aave-v4-spoke-revenue}, tuning Target-HF engines in a single-directional setup forces a strict trade-off between borrower UX and liquidation reliability. Our two-sided approach adds a recovery side to this design: positive-spread, reborrow-funded buybacks repair borrower collateral during rebounds. We find the recovery side is value-accretive (and not merely neutral) only when its reborrow is bounded by a health-factor floor; this re-leverage guard, rather than the buyback itself, is the load-bearing design element. Meanwhile, Curve crvUSD emphasizes continuous band migration and soft-liquidation with AMM-mediated execution \cite{curve-crvusd,curve-llamma,egorov2023crvusd}.

Empirical evidence on liquidation dynamics and cascading effects in DeFi further motivates tail-aware evaluation: prior work documents liquidation concentration, fragility around collateral boundaries, and protocol-level design externalities \cite{qin2021liquidations,perez2021knifeedge,gudgeon2020loanable}.

From a mechanism-design view, LLTV/LTV execution with target-HF sizing can be interpreted as a bounded control over collateral exposure. This offers an engineering advantage for protocol operators who require transparent stress testing and parameter governance.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Relative to hard-threshold liquidations, endogenous partial liquidation provides smoother deleveraging and explicit recovery semantics. Relative to AMM-coupled soft liquidation systems, this design emphasizes deterministic trigger rules and separated accounting of inventory and execution logic. The contribution here is mechanism structure and operational clarity, not a claim of universal dominance.

\section{Limitations and Evaluation Roadmap}
The results rest on several explicit assumptions, which we state as scope rather than bury as caveats. (i) \textbf{Data}: the evidence base is two assets (ETH, BTC) over roughly one year; magnitudes are indicative, not externally validated across assets, venues, or regimes. (ii) \textbf{Execution}: trades clear at the oracle price with \emph{zero slippage} and no latency microstructure; since slippage $\sigma$ adds to the per-liquidation friction (\S3), realistic execution makes the re-leverage floor more valuable, not less, but also taxes the sell side we model as frictionless beyond the bonus. (iii) \textbf{Metric}: borrower loss is an opportunity cost relative to passive holding, marked in USD at the terminal price; as \S3 shows this is numeraire-dependent, so the USD figures should be read alongside the collateral-restoration ratio rather than alone. (iv) \textbf{Selection}: the headline candidate is chosen in-sample. (v) \textbf{Initial health}: favorable bad-debt results assume positions start healthy ($\mathrm{CR}_0=1.30$); positions already underwater at observation cannot be repaired by partial liquidation and remain a residual bad-debt source, and discrete-time replay can mask short-lived gap-driven bad-debt peaks. Parameter governance for live positions (safe migration across policy updates) is a further open problem. Future work should prioritize multi-asset/multi-period validation, market-impact-aware execution, out-of-sample candidate selection, and adversarial stress tests around the trigger boundary.

\paragraph{Evaluation roadmap.}
A complete empirical evaluation should cross LLTV levels, close-factor slope and bounds, trigger hysteresis (buyback risk-guard bandwidth), market regime class, recovery policy, and redemption toggles. The relevant outputs are restoration--profit frontiers, event turnover and slippage sensitivity, tail bad-debt statistics, and depeg half-life proxies. This design makes risk-management trade-offs directly measurable.

\section{Conclusion}
Target-HF LLTV/LTV soft liquidation with a guarded band-restoration leg provides a practical, auditable risk-control primitive for volatile lending markets. Target-HF partial liquidation Pareto-dominates fixed-close-factor liquidation---lower bad debt and no-worse borrower loss---a result that holds out of sample on $15$ assets across 2019--2024 (the deleveraging baseline). Solvency is a governance frontier rather than an absolute: an early, pre-emptive trigger drives out-of-sample worst-case bad debt to a few hundred USD at a modest borrower-loss cost, while a loss-minimizing late trigger leaves a residual. But that deleveraging strength is Aave's, not ours. Measured against the right baseline---Aave v4's sell-only target-HF engine---our contribution, the guarded buyback leg, is honestly a \emph{modest, safe, near-free option}: it restores strictly more collateral (an accounting guarantee), adds no bad debt, induces almost no extra liquidation, and delivers a small, conditional USD benefit that accrues mainly to long-horizon, asset-valuing borrowers. We make no claim that it dominates the Aave baseline in dollars; we claim it is a safe restoration option the sell-only baseline lacks, with the re-leverage guard as the ingredient that makes it safe. Its value being preference-dependent, the right deployment is a per-borrower opt-in module: collateralized borrowers with different numeraires, horizons, and asset convictions self-select, and because the guard keeps the option risk-neutral to the protocol, offering it to some never burdens those who decline. The contribution is the restoration leg and its quantity rule: recovery must be solvency-governed, not lot-matched, because a reborrow-funded buyback lowers the health factor and a naive lot-matched rule re-levers the position into ping-pong re-liquidation---and liquidation is not free, so each induced cycle burns the liquidation bonus (and slippage), turning an unguarded leg into a friction-bleeding leveraged long that can be net-negative even in collateral terms. Bounded by a health-factor floor, the leg never induces extra liquidation and never adds bad debt, and an exact identity characterizes its value: it restores strictly more collateral on every path ($\Delta\rho>0$), while its USD outcome $\sum_i q_i(P_T-P_i^{\text{buy}})$ is a band-bottom leveraged long---unconditionally favorable for a borrower who values the collateral asset or holds a long-horizon conviction that it recovers above the band-bottom buy prices, and a horizon-dependent bet for one marking to USD. The re-leverage floor is thus both the safety guarantee and an explicit governance dial over the restoration--re-leverage trade-off, and is, to our knowledge, a previously unaddressed ingredient of two-sided liquidation-restoration design. These findings, regenerated end-to-end by the released scripts, establish a reproducible baseline for external validation on broader market datasets.

\section{Event-Engine Pseudocode}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialize loan state $C_0=D\,\mathrm{CR}_0/P_0$, $U_0=0$, $\Pi_0=0$, and policy parameters $(\mathrm{LLTV},\mathrm{HF}^{\star},b,\eta,\delta,\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}},s)$.
  \item For each new price $P_t$, compute $\mathrm{LTV}_t$ and evaluate liquidation eligibility first:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if $\mathrm{LTV}_t\ge\mathrm{LLTV}$ (require $\mathrm{HF}^{\star}>\mathrm{LT}/\mathrm{LLTV}$ for feasibility), compute target repayment $\Delta D_t^{\star}=D_t(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{HF}_t)/(\mathrm{HF}^{\star}-\mathrm{LT}(1+b))$, clip by debt/collateral constraints,
    and execute $q_t^{\text{sell}}=(1+b)\Delta D_t/P_t$.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Evaluate buyback risk-guard condition:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if $\mathrm{LTV}_t\le\mathrm{LLTV}+\delta$ and some open band level satisfies $P_i^{\text{sell}}>(1+s)P_t$, reborrow at most $B_t^{\star}=\max(0,(\mathrm{LT}\,C_tP_t-\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}D_t)/(\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}-\mathrm{LT}))$ (post-buy $\mathrm{HF}\ge\mathrm{HF}^{\text{floor}}$), and allocate the bought quantity across the band from the top down---not matched one-to-one to a sold lot.
  \end{itemize}
  \item For terminal states, allow protective buyback even when incremental trade P\&L is negative.
  \item Update debt and inventories, then recompute derived risk metrics (HF, LTV, CR, bad debt).
\end{enumerate}
